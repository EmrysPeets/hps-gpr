\section{Toy Generation and Validation Studies}
\label{sec:toys}

\subsection{Scope of the current validation campaign}

Unless stated otherwise, the toy studies summarized here correspond to the 95\%
confidence-level workflow with blind width $1.64\,\sigma_m$ and \num{10000} toys per
mass-strength point. The staged suites now cover the fully unblinded 2015 sample, the
2016 10\% development sample, the 2021 1\% validation stream, and the current
shared-$\eps^2$ combinations built from those inputs. Throughout this section, the
combined interpretation is focused on the mass intervals where multiple data sets
contribute meaningfully to the prompt-resonance search, with the two-dataset
2015+2016 overlap anchored near \SIrange{42}{130}{MeV} and the three-dataset examples
drawn from the common subset used by the current injection configs.

Many of the toy-validation figures were produced before the final adoption of the
leakage-retaining signal template and before the future likelihood refinements
mentioned in Section~\ref{sec:methodology}. They are nevertheless already informative
for the statistical questions that matter most: closure, bias, pull calibration,
coverage, and the logic of the combined extraction. Where newer note-local products are
available, especially for the no-refit three-dataset pseudoexperiment displays and the
projected reach discussion, those updated exports are used explicitly below.

\subsection{Three toy-generation modes}

The validation program uses three complementary pseudoexperiment constructions.

\subsubsection{Analytic functional-form pseudoexperiments}

The first class of toys starts from an analytic fit to the smooth $e^+e^-$
invariant-mass spectrum and fluctuates pseudo-data around that closed-form background
model. These studies isolate the question of gross spectral smoothness from the later
question of GP conditioning. They are therefore useful as the first closure test before
the full inference chain is exercised. Figure~\ref{fig:analytic-fit-publication-overview}
now packages the publication-facing overview year by year: the candidate-family
overlay appears on the left of each row, and the selected primary fit together with
the generated toy-ensemble mean appears on the right. The current retuning pass is
organized around one portable shifted
sigmoid$\times$power$\times$exponential family plus a normalized tail modifier, with
generalized-gamma variants retained as cross-checks in the lower-mass years. In each
left-hand panel, the legend reports the same bin-integral
$\chi^2/\mathrm{ndof}$ convention used by the macro when ranking the candidate
functions. The present seeds reach the target selection region of
$\chi^2/\mathrm{ndof}<2$ for the 2016 and 2021 primaries, while the 2015 turn-on
remains provisionally acceptable at $\chi^2/\mathrm{ndof}<4$ pending a later dedicated
low-mass iteration.

The corrected toy workflow now keeps three mass intervals explicit in every export:
the full histogram support used for toy normalization, the fit interval used to
determine the analytic parameters, and the scan interval passed to the search code.
Pseudo-data are generated bin-by-bin from Poisson means equal to the bin integrals of
the selected analytic fit after rescaling that fit to the observed event count on the
full support range. This avoids both the center-value approximation and the earlier
fixed-total construction in which every toy inherited exactly the same event count.
It also leaves the blind-window slice un-renormalized: the search interval inherits
the normalization implied by the full histogram rather than by an isolated scan-window
fit. The 2015 study keeps the scan hypotheses at \SIrange{20}{130}{MeV}; the 2016
toy-validation pass is now displayed with the updated \SIrange{42}{210}{MeV} scan
window to suppress the low-mass mismodeling that biased the earlier 35--42~MeV region;
and the 2021 row remains a provisional 1\% placeholder until the next export is
frozen. The right-hand panels in Figure~\ref{fig:analytic-fit-publication-overview}
show the most direct visualization of the corrected generation rule: the toy mean
follows the selected fit while the toy totals fluctuate around, rather than being
pinned to, the observed support-range event count.

\subsubsection{Conditional blind-window GP toys}

The fastest toy mode keeps the sideband-trained GP fixed at a given test mass. The GP
fit is performed once on the sidebands, the blind-window mean vector and covariance are
constructed from that fit, a background realization is drawn, signal is added, and the
result is Poisson-sampled. This construction is ideal for high-statistics expected-band
studies and for testing the response of the extraction fit under the posterior
background model preferred by the real data.

\subsubsection{Full procedural GP-mean/refit pseudoexperiments}

The most realistic toy mode generates a full pseudoexperiment from the GP-derived
background expectation, injects signal with the full Gaussian template, and then reruns
the GP sideband-to-blind prediction on the toy before signal extraction. Here ``GP
mean'' means the full-range propagated count-space mean obtained from the sideband-fit
GP model of the observed spectrum. That propagated mean is used as the toy-truth
background source over the full histogram support; the toy is then fluctuated, signal
is added, and the ordinary mass-scan workflow refits the GP on the toy sidebands at
each scanned mass. This is the appropriate mode for diagnosing signal absorption,
because it directly tests how much of the injected signal may be absorbed by the
refitted background model or by the profiled nuisance treatment. The phrase
``refit-on-toy'' is therefore literal: the search stage does not reuse the original
data-conditioned blind-window prediction.

\subsubsection{Fixed-total GP-propagated toy-scan study}

The new background-only validation study used for the individual 2015, 2016, and 2021
checks starts from a single full-range GP fit to the observed spectrum of that
dataset, takes the propagated count-space mean over the full histogram support, and
generates \num{1000} pseudoexperiments from that mean while preserving the exact total
event count of the original full input histogram range. The resulting toy histogram is
then passed through the ordinary mass-scan workflow, so the GP is refit on the toy
sidebands at every mass hypothesis with the same $1.64\,\sigma_d$ exclusion used in
the study configuration. In other words, the toy source is GP-derived, but the scan
itself is not simplified or shortcut. In the notation used on the plots, this mode is
described as ``GP propagated mean / fixed-total / refit-on-toy'' to make all three
procedural ingredients explicit: the toy truth is the propagated GP mean, the full
histogram total count is held fixed, and the sideband GP is refit on each toy before
the extraction step is repeated.

This study is designed to be compared directly with the functional-form toy scans. The
functional-form ensemble tests closure with respect to an analytic smooth-spectrum
ansatz, whereas the fixed-total GP ensemble tests whether the sideband-refit GP
machinery remains well behaved when the toy truth is itself the propagated GP mean.
Because the ensemble size is large, the merged validation plots are summarized by the
toy median together with central 68\% and 95\% bands rather than by overlaying all
\num{1000} toy curves. The same fixed-total toy source also feeds a matched
refit-on-toy expected upper-limit band, but that band should be interpreted as a
background-modeling validation product rather than as the canonical expected-band
overlay for the observed limit. The first note-local pilot generated directly from the
merged CSV is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:toy-scan-validation-2015-gpmean-pilot}. It uses
\num{10} GP-mean pseudoexperiments from the 2015 study, so it should be read as a
low-statistics procedural check rather than as a frozen closure statement. Even at
that pilot scale, however, the figure is already useful for verifying that the merged
plots carry the correct source description, right-side metadata panel, upper-left
upper-limit legend, and thinned toy-index summary axis that will be used in the full
rerun.

\subsection{Asimov calibration and background-only pseudoexperiments}

When toy strengths are specified in units of $\sigma_A$, the package first constructs a
reference extraction uncertainty $\sigma_{A,\mathrm{ref}}(m)$ by fitting the local
signal-strength model to a background-only reference spectrum,
\begin{equation}
\left(\hat A_{\mathrm{ref}},\sigma_{A,\mathrm{ref}}\right)
=
\mathcal{F}\!\left[\bm{n}_{\mathrm{ref}};\,\bm{b}(m),\bm{C}(m),\bm{w}(m)\right],
\end{equation}
where $\bm{b}(m)$ and $\bm{C}(m)$ are the GP-predicted blind-window mean and covariance,
and $\bm{w}(m)$ is the full-range Gaussian signal template restricted to the blinded
bins. The injected strength axis is then defined by
\begin{equation}
A_{\mathrm{inj}}(m) = Z_{\mathrm{inj}}\,\sigma_{A,\mathrm{ref}}(m).
\end{equation}

In \textbf{Asimov mode}, the reference spectrum is deterministic,
\begin{equation}
n_{\mathrm{ref},i} = b_i,
\end{equation}
so $\sigma_{A,\mathrm{ref}}$ measures the nominal local sensitivity implied by the
fitted background model. In \textbf{Poisson mode}, the reference spectrum is first
fluctuated,
\begin{equation}
n_{\mathrm{ref},i} \sim \mathrm{Pois}\!\left(b_i\right),
\end{equation}
and the extracted $\sigma_{A,\mathrm{ref}}$ then inherits an extra layer of
background-only sampling noise. The completed 95\% CL studies summarized here use the
Asimov convention because it cleanly separates the definition of the injected signal
axis from the later ensemble fluctuations of the toy study itself
\cite{Cowan1998,Cowan2011}.

This distinction matters for the later profile-likelihood interpretation. The Asimov
construction defines the expected sensitivity scale, while the background-only
pseudoexperiments are what test coverage, bias, and finite-sample departures from the
asymptotic approximation. Put differently: the Asimov dataset calibrates the injection
axis, but the pseudoexperiments remain the mechanism that profiles the background model
and measures how realistic background-only fluctuations propagate through the
extraction chain.

\subsection{Poisson versus multinomial signal injection}

Signal injection is distinct from the choice of $\sigma_A$ reference. In
\textbf{Poisson-total injection}, each bin receives a Poisson draw with mean
$A_{\mathrm{inj}}w_i$, so the realized total signal fluctuates from toy to toy. In
\textbf{multinomial fixed-total injection}, the total number of injected events is
fixed and only the bin-to-bin allocation fluctuates. Since the present signal template
is normalized on the full histogram range, $A_{\mathrm{inj}}$ denotes the total local
signal yield and only the fraction $f_{\mathrm{blind}}A_{\mathrm{inj}}$ is expected to
appear inside the blinded bins for the nominal $1.64\,\sigma_m$ window.

This distinction matters when interpreting uncertainty bars on validation plots. Only
Poisson-total injection produces a genuine horizontal spread in the realized injected
strength. Under fixed-total multinomial injection the horizontal coordinate is exact.

\subsection{Refit versus no-refit extraction displays}

The note now distinguishes two reviewer-facing pseudoexperiment tests because they are
answering different questions. In the \textbf{no-refit} display, the GP sideband model
is fitted once on the real data and then held fixed while a background realization is
drawn in the blind window. This is the cleanest closure test of whether the extraction
fit can recover an injected signal when the background prediction is treated as the
correct local model. In the \textbf{full-refit} display, the toy is generated over the
full mass range and the GP is refit on the toy sidebands before the blind-window
extraction is repeated. That second test is slower and more aggressive, but it is also
the direct diagnostic for signal absorption by the refitted background model.

These tests should therefore not be judged by the same visual criterion. Agreement
between the no-refit pseudoexperiment display and the observed-data display is expected
and desirable because both are using the same sideband-conditioned background
description. The full-refit display is instead meant to stress the nuisance-model
freedom. When it visibly degrades the extracted signal, the correct interpretation is
not that the no-refit test was ``too easy,'' but that the full procedural toy is
probing how much the sideband refit can absorb signal-like structure. That is exactly
why both views should be documented in the publication workflow.

\subsection{Pull, coverage, and significance-calibration diagnostics}

The central ensemble-level validation quantities are the pull,
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{pull} = \frac{\hat A - A_{\mathrm{inj}}}{\sigma_A},
\end{equation}
its mean and width as a function of mass and injected strength, and the empirical
coverage of the nominal one- and two-standard-deviation intervals. These are standard
closure diagnostics in HEP statistical analyses because they test, respectively,
whether the estimator is biased, whether the quoted uncertainty is correctly calibrated,
and whether the reported intervals achieve the claimed frequentist coverage
\cite{Cowan1998,Cowan2011}.

The note now separates two uses of these diagnostics. The figures immediately below are
generated with the GP-based injection/extraction pseudoexperiment workflow described
earlier in this section, so they should be read as GP-conditioned closure and calibration
checks of the extraction layer. The later functional-form closure block reuses the same
summary observables, but with pseudo-data whose smooth-spectrum truth comes from the
analytic toy seeds rather than from a GP-generated background ensemble.

\subsubsection{GP-generated pseudoexperiment diagnostics}

In practice the validation outputs are organized so that each product answers one
statistical question cleanly. The linearity and bias panels test estimator closure;
the pull-width and coverage panels test uncertainty calibration; the pull-mean and
pull-width heatmaps localize those effects in the mass-strength plane; the
pull-versus-mass summaries compress the dominant mass trends into one view; and the
$\Delta Z$ residual plots test significance calibration for both the single-dataset
and shared-$\eps^2$ workflows.

For the present study, the pull-versus-mass plots are the most compact summary because
they display both the pull mean and pull width at once. In the overlap region

\subsubsection{Functional-form closure studies}

The functional-form closure studies answer a related but distinct question: whether the
blind-window extraction closes when the toy truth is a smooth analytic spectrum rather
than a GP-derived background realization. The plots included here were produced with the
functional-form \texttt{inject} workflow, not with \texttt{toy-scan}: each toy starts
from a pre-generated functional-form histogram, the nominal signal strength
$A_{\mathrm{inj}}$ or $Z_{\mathrm{inj}}$ defines the mean injected yield, and the
realized signal contribution is then Poisson-sampled template-bin by template-bin so the
total injected signal fluctuates toy by toy around that mean rather than being held
fixed exactly.

This distinction matters for the sideband question. In the \texttt{funcform100}
injection configs used for the figures below, \texttt{inj\_refit\_gp\_on\_toy=false}, so
these panels are a \textbf{fixed-background / no-refit} closure test on top of a
functional-form toy truth. They validate whether the local extraction fit recovers the
injected signal once the sideband-conditioned GP prediction for that toy is held fixed.
They do \emph{not} by themselves prove closure of the full toy-by-toy sideband-refit scan.
That stronger statement belongs either to the dedicated functional-form
\texttt{toy-scan} ladder or to an injection workflow rerun with
\texttt{inj\_refit\_gp\_on\_toy=true}. The separate GP-mean/refit pseudoexperiments
therefore remain the complementary absorption stress test, not an automatic consequence
of these particular functional-form injection plots.

The pull and $Z$-calibration summaries should also not be expected to match
point-by-point. The pull compresses the mean and width of
$(\hat A-A_{\mathrm{inj}})/\sigma_A$, while the significance residual uses
$\Delta Z=\hat Z-Z_{\mathrm{inj}}$ with $\hat Z=\hat A/\sigma_A$ but
$Z_{\mathrm{inj}}=A_{\mathrm{inj}}/\sigma_{A,\mathrm{ref}}$. Since the extracted toy
significance uses the toy-level $\sigma_A$ whereas the injected significance is defined
with the reference scale $\sigma_{A,\mathrm{ref}}$, modest differences between the pull
curves and the $\Delta Z$ curves are expected even when the study is behaving well.

\subsection{Combined search-power studies}

The combined-search-power figures illustrate why a shared-$\eps^2$ combination can
outperform any one constituent data set alone. The key point is that the combined
injection is not defined by adding the same number of signal events to each data set.
Instead, a common injected $\eps^2$ is mapped into dataset-specific total signal yields
through the conversion factors $K_d(m)$ and then interpreted relative to the
dataset-specific $\sigma_{A,\mathrm{ref}}$. As a result, a combined ``5$\sigma$'' or
``7$\sigma$'' injection means a common-coupling significance in the combined statistic,
not the same per-dataset significance in each constituent channel.

The updated search-power plotter is now configured for both the historical
two-dataset export and the current three-dataset scenarios documented in the README.
In particular, the same stacked layout can be rerendered for intuitive
``1$\sigma$ + 1$\sigma$ + 1$\sigma$'' and ``1$\sigma$ + 2$\sigma$ + 3$\sigma$''
shared-coupling tests once the corresponding injection-summary CSVs are bundled in the
note workspace. The panels shown here therefore remain provisional in their numerical
content, but they already use the publication-facing top-bottom layout and the final
inverse-variance bookkeeping. Figure~\ref{fig:combined-search-power} then shows both
the combined search-power scan and the constituent-dataset allocations needed to reach
a target shared significance.

\subsection{Projected unblinded reach in \texorpdfstring{$\eps^2$}{epsilon2}}

The same inverse-variance logic can be used for a simple full-luminosity projection.
For the current study this is implemented directly at the level of the dataset-specific
$\eps^2$ curves: each constituent curve is scaled by $1/\sqrt{L}$, and the projected
shared-coupling sensitivity is then recomputed with the same inverse-variance
combination logic used elsewhere in the note. The resulting projection is shown with
the three-dataset results in Section~\ref{sec:results-combined-151621-1pct}, where it
is most naturally interpreted as part of the physics-facing combined reach.

\subsection{Combined extraction displays}

The common-signal extraction displays are the most transparent way to visualize the
shared-$\eps^2$ construction. The top row shows individual single-dataset
pseudoexperiments. The lower panel shows the combined Gaussian signal representation
constructed from the same injected coupling. For the reviewer-facing note, the main
illustrations are now the no-refit pseudoexperiments with a five-standard-deviation
injected shared signal at \SIlist{40;80;120}{MeV}. Those examples are deliberately
shown without toy-by-toy GP retraining because they are meant to test extraction
closure under the sideband-conditioned background model and to show, mass by mass, how
the shared coupling is partitioned among 2015, 2016, and 2021.
